# CLEAN_FIELD_MANUAL - Dog Gone Clean grooming and trailer SOPs

The hands-on operating manual for the work itself: grooming craft, equipment, power, climate,
the trailer, supplies, and maintenance. The Oracle holds business rules; this file holds how
the job is physically done. Most of it was absorbed from `the_oracle_journal` (Paul's original
voice-dictated journal on Drive) on 2026-05-24.

This is a saleability asset (per the Oracle's `clean_stays_saleable`): a buyer or a new
operator needs these SOPs to run the trailer without Paul. Keep it current as methods change,
and keep the "because" on every entry, since the reasoning is the part no one can re-derive.
No em dashes anywhere (`scripts/check.py` enforces this).

## Grooming craft

**Photograph and step back before finishing.** When a dog looks done, take a photo and look at
it, and also step back to arm's length or more. Because the camera and the distance both reveal
flaws you cannot see while staring at the dog up close, and small adjustments made then make
the finished dog look noticeably better.

**Nails: rotary tool only, no clippers.** Shorten and sculpt nails with the rotary tool alone,
skipping clippers entirely. Because it gets nails much shorter than clippers do, causes far less
bleeding, and leaves smooth edges in one step.

**Foot trim shortcut with a #7 blade.** On almost every dog, run a #7 blade on the underside of
the foot from the pad down the center path, then down both sides of the foot, then the tips of
the toes (more on top if the client wants the feet shorter). Finish quickly with scissors.
Because this gets the foot most of the way there and saves a large amount of scissoring time.

**Scissoring long-hair feet.** Reach between the toes with a pincer motion, pull the hair
straight up, and cut it at the level of the toes. Because hair between the toes is the longest
and grows back unevenly; if it is not pulled up and cut off, the foot looks wild and unbalanced
within days.

**Jaw-to-chest separation on long-coat dogs (for example Shih Tzu).** Clip a very narrow band
with a #7 blade under the jaw from ear to ear. Because a narrow band gives a clean separation
between the lower jaw and the chest while staying narrow enough that no one notices a band was
shaved.

**Back dew claws: extreme caution with clippers.** If a dog has rear dew claws, use extreme
care with clippers, especially a #7 blade. Because the slightest touch of a #7 on a dew claw
makes it trickle blood for 24 hours or more (not dangerous, but a long mess).

**Nail care, sudden resistance on one leg.** If a dog is cooperative and then suddenly resists
on a particular leg, treat it as a possible problem with that leg (arthritis, soreness, an
injury). Be gentle and try a different hold or angle. Because the sudden change is a signal you
may be causing discomfort, not defiance.

**Uncooperative dog: turn it 180 degrees.** For a few dogs that refuse to cooperate during nail
care, turning the dog to face the other direction settles them right down. Reason unknown, but
it works often enough to try.

**Toe stretch (a kindness).** If a dog's toes are tight together laterally, gently spread them a
little, feeling for resistance. Because tight toes seem to feel good to stretch and the dog
relaxes afterward. Not grooming, just a nice thing to do.

## Drying

**Always chase the wettest spot.** Keep the dryer moving and aim it at the wettest part of the
dog; never linger on one area, and never dry an area that is already less wet than another.
Because staying in one place wastes time. Take a break between passes if needed.

**Dehumidifier is a drying tool, not just comfort.** Run the dehumidifier to hold the interior
around 30 percent humidity regardless of the weather outside. Because dry air pulls water off
the coat while you blow-dry and cuts drying time by more than half.

**Multi-dog wash order.** When more than one dog is in the trailer at once, wash the short-coat
dog first, give it a quick dryer pass, and set it on towels. Because in 30 percent humidity it
finishes drying on its own while you groom the long-coat dog, so its drying time is effectively
free.

## The Hurricane Bath (the wash system)

Clean's signature equipment and a real competitive moat (`dig_the_moat`): a wash system Paul
designed and built, more powerful than the commercial units it resembles. Treat the build below
as proprietary. It is captured here so the business owns it, but it is NOT for the public
website. The marketing showcase (`marketing/hurricane_bath_showcase.md`) sells what the bath
does, never how it is built.

- **Dual-pump core.** Two heavy-duty submersible pumps sit in an external 5-gallon bucket linked
  to the main freshwater supply (5-gallon or 55-gallon). Because two heavy-duty submersibles move
  far more water than the standard low-pressure RV pump, the system blasts the volume needed to
  cut through thick coats fast.
- **Command valve.** A custom directional valve mounted on the wall toggles two modes with a
  flip:
  - **Recirculation mode:** recycles the water and shampoo mix through the tub for aggressive,
    deep, saturating cleaning.
  - **Pump-out mode:** diverts water out of the system for fast, effortless draining.
- **Open (data gaps):** pump make/model and power draw, the valve part, the plumbing layout, and
  the real flow rate. Fill from the actual rig; do not guess.

Operation and upkeep of the wash and drain lines is under Maintenance routine (the bathing
machine flush).

## Power and generators

**Two generators, for redundancy.** Run two generators rather than one large unit (currently
two Predator 5000s). Because if one fails you can limp through the rest of the day on the other
instead of losing the day. Predator 5000s have been reliable and quiet where other generators
were not.

**Know the load, wire it safely.** Know each generator's capacity and what you are plugging into
it. Plug everything directly in, no daisy-chaining, and use heavy-duty extension cords for
high-draw items. Because high-current devices need cords that can carry the load.

**Run with headroom.** Keep more power available than you need; do not run a generator at full
capacity constantly. Because constant full load wears it out faster.

**Throw power at problems.** Never skip a tool or device that makes the job easier or faster
just because of a power shortfall. Buy a third or fourth generator if that is what it takes.
Because the work earned by the tool is worth more than the cost of more power.

**Dryer choice: Shernbao orange dual-motor.** Preferred dryer is the orange Shernbao with dual
motors. Because it is powerful and its soft-start ramps up slowly, which avoids the surge that
trips breakers on the generator.

**Rotary tools: keep many, rotate, replace freely.** Use Chinese-knockoff rotary tools over
Dremel, and keep several on hand. Because the knockoffs innovated (USB-C charging, larger
drums) while Dremel kept shipping the same micro-USB design, and having multiples means you grab
the next one instead of fumbling. They weaken gradually without you noticing, so rotate through
them and throw away any that feel weak (they are cheap). Replace a worn sandpaper drum head the
moment it dulls; keep a bag of pre-loaded fresh drums so a swap takes seconds rather than
unscrewing and re-sanding a head mid-job.

**Clipper vacuum: near-indispensable in a trailer.** Use a clipper vacuum that sucks hair away
as you clip. Because a confined trailer fills with hair fast, and the airflow also keeps blades
cool against friction heat. The Hanvey Shop-Vac conversion kit attaches a clipper to any
standard vacuum and gives more options than the expensive built-in systems.

## Climate control

**Portable dual-hose AC over a rooftop unit.** Cool the trailer with a portable dual-hose air
conditioner, not a rooftop install. Because any AC eventually fails, and a portable unit can be
replaced same-day at any big-box store while a rooftop unit needs professional installation and
lead time. Dual-hose matters: it draws and exhausts outside air, so it does not ingest the dog
hair and filth that clogs a single-hose unit and kills its cooling.

## Trailer and vehicle

**Level the trailer for drainage.** On uneven ground where the nose sits low, use the jack to
level it. Because a level trailer lets the tub drain by gravity. Put the jack back up before
driving; if you forget you will feel heavy resistance the moment you try to pull away.

**Jacked-up or uncoupled means no trailer lights.** The trailer lights will not work when the
trailer is jacked up or when the coupling is off the hitch ball. This is a grounding behavior,
not a fault; the lights return once it is recoupled and lowered. Do not troubleshoot a problem
that is not there.

**Secure everything before driving, especially AC and dehumidifier.** Strap down everything in
the trailer, with the air conditioner and dehumidifier as the top priority. Because if either
tips it can be damaged, and at minimum you must wait hours for the oil to settle back before it
is safe to run again.

## Supplies and backups

**Back up nearly everything; the system must not shut down.** Because gear under heavy daily use
constantly wears out or breaks, and being down for days while a replacement ships would do
catastrophic financial damage.

- **Critical and slow to replace (days of lead time): dryer, clippers, clipper blades.** Keep a
  ready spare for each (two blades is better than one). When you use one up, throw it away and
  reorder immediately so the replacement arrives before you need it again.
- **Replaceable same-day but still kept as a spare: the clipper/central vacuum.** Keep one
  waiting so a failure costs minutes to swap, not a shopping trip mid-day, since these always
  fail when you have no time to shop.

**Gas: never run out.** Carry a minimum of two five-gallon cans; both need not be full, but at
least one is never empty. Keep two to three gallons spare beyond whatever is already in the
generators before starting any appointment. Because running out costs money and time and looks
foolish, and it is never worth the gamble.

**Charging station.** Keep one dedicated charging spot near a power strip (currently a lidded
bucket) with enough USB-C cords to charge every device that might need it at the same time.
Because a missing or occupied cord is a friction point that leaves a device dead when you need
it. Keep it charging-only, not a catch-all junk drawer.

## Maintenance routine

**Bathing machine: keep it clean, flush after idle periods.** The bathing machine must stay
clean because the whole job depends on it. After any extended period unused, cycle water with a
spot of bleach through both the wash and the drain functions, then rinse both thoroughly with
fresh water so no bleach smell is left behind. Because dirty water lingers in the lines and
turns foul while idle, and no one wants their dog smelling like bleach.

**Generators: check threaded parts for vibration loosening.** Add a pass over the generator's
threaded parts (for example the screw-on waterproof covers over the breaker switches) to
routine maintenance. Because vibration backs them loose and a small part can drop off and be
lost without you noticing.

**Tires and bearings: set an interval.** Decide and follow an interval for checking trailer tire
pressure, rotating the tires, and servicing the wheel bearings. Because keeping the tires and
bearings in good order is what keeps the trailer moving. (Interval still to be defined; see
open items.)
